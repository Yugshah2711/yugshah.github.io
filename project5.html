Modeling Neuroplasticity And Synaptic Dynamics In Parkinson's Disease : Integrating Brain Activity and Nerve Pathways

Special thanks to Dr. Michaelle Ntala Mayalu for her support and guidance for the execution of this project.

Project Overview

This study presents an advanced computational model aimed at exploring the intricate dynamics of neuronal activity and calcium homeostasis in Parkinson’s Disease (PD). By integrating inputs from both the central and peripheral nervous systems, this model accurately reflects the complexities associated with the condition. Our research thoroughly examines neuronal excitability, connectivity, and intracellular signaling, revealing the essential role of dopamine in regulating brain circuits. Specifically, it highlights dopamine's impact on motor control and neural communication within the basal ganglia. The loss of dopaminergic neurons characteristic of PD results in considerable disruptions in the functional patterns of critical brain regions, which contributes to classic motor symptoms such as bradykinesia and rigidity. Furthermore, the model emphasizes the importance of calcium regulation, illustrating how its dysregulation can lead to neuronal damage and underscoring the necessity of maintaining calcium balance for optimal brain health. By offering a comprehensive multi-scale perspective on the pathophysiology of PD, this research not only enhances our understanding of the disease but also paves the way for the development of innovative diagnostic and therapeutic strategies. These advancements hold the promise of significantly improving treatment modalities and enhancing the quality of life for patients.

Key Contributions

- Developed a sophisticated computational model to simulate neuronal dynamics and calcium homeostasis in Parkinson’s Disease (PD).
- Investigated the effects of dopaminergic neuron degeneration on motor control and neural communication within the basal ganglia.
- Analyzed the interplay between calcium regulation and neuronal excitability, revealing its significance in the pathophysiology of PD.
- Identified critical factors leading to disruptions in functional patterns of key brain regions associated with PD symptoms.
- Explored the impact of both central and peripheral nervous system inputs on neuronal activity in the context of PD.
- Provided insights into the role of dopamine in modulating brain circuits and its implications for motor symptoms like bradykinesia and rigidity.
- Highlighted the necessity of maintaining calcium balance for neuronal health and the potential for calcium dysregulation to cause further neuronal damage.
- Paved the way for future research into diagnostic and therapeutic strategies aimed at mitigating the effects of PD.
- Enhanced understanding of the multi-scale interactions that contribute to PD pathophysiology.
- Offered new perspectives on treatment approaches that could improve patient outcomes and quality of life.

Figure 1: Basal Ganglia Pathways

This diagram provides a comprehensive overview of the basal ganglia pathways involved in motor control. It illustrates both the direct and indirect pathways, highlighting the role of key structures such as the substantia nigra, striatum, globus pallidus externus (GPe), subthalamic nucleus (STN), and the thalamus. The diagram emphasizes the interactions between excitatory (Glu) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmitters, along with the modulation by dopamine (DA). Understanding these pathways is crucial for elucidating the dysfunctions that occur in Parkinson's Disease, where the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons leads to significant motor impairments. This visual representation aids in comprehending how disturbances in these circuits contribute to the hallmark symptoms of the disease, including bradykinesia and rigidity.

Figure 1: Membrane Potential and Input Current

This figure presents the relationship between external input current and membrane potential over time. The upper plot illustrates the external input current applied to the neuron, showing brief spikes indicating how the current influences neuronal activity. The lower plot depicts the corresponding membrane potential, which fluctuates in response to the input current. This dynamic illustrates the neuron's excitability and the effect of synaptic inputs on its firing rate, highlighting the critical role of current modulation in neuronal signaling.

Figure 2: Hodgkin-Huxley Neuron Model

This figure depicts the Hodgkin-Huxley neuron model, showcasing the changes in membrane potential over time alongside the applied external current. The upper plot represents the membrane potential, which exhibits action potentials characterized by rapid depolarization and subsequent repolarization phases. The lower plot shows the external current as a function of time, demonstrating the current pulses that stimulate the neuron. This model serves as a foundational framework for understanding action potential dynamics, emphasizing how electrical signals propagate through neurons, which is essential for understanding neural communication.

Figure 4: Membrane Potentials of Two Neurons

This figure compares the membrane potentials of two neurons over a period of time. It highlights the difference in firing patterns and the variation in action potential amplitude between neurons. The figure provides insights into the dynamics of neuronal excitability and the timing of action potentials, which are essential for understanding neural network behavior and communication.

Figure 5: Calcium Concentrations in Neurons

This plot demonstrates the intracellular calcium concentration changes in two neurons over time. Calcium plays a vital role in synaptic transmission and plasticity, and the gradual increase depicted in the figure showcases calcium’s involvement in neuronal signaling. Understanding these fluctuations can help predict how neurons respond to stimuli and maintain homeostasis.

Figure 6: Calcium Gating Variables for Neurons

This figure displays the calcium gating variables for two neurons, representing the channels' opening and closing states over time. The variations in the gating variables influence the flow of calcium ions, affecting neural excitability and signaling pathways. This plot is essential for understanding the feedback mechanisms involved in calcium dynamics within neurons.

Figure 7: Parkinson’s Disease Model - Network Activity

This figure showcases the activity levels across different brain regions in a simulated Parkinson’s Disease state. The top panel represents input from the cortex, followed by the striatum, GPi/SNr, and the thalamus output. The plot highlights disruptions in the normal firing patterns, where prolonged activation and irregular bursts reflect the impaired motor control commonly associated with Parkinson’s Disease. These patterns demonstrate how alterations in neuronal communication impact motor function in the disease.

Figure 8: Healthy Network Activity for Comparison

This figure provides a comparative view of the network activity in a healthy brain model. Each panel tracks the firing activity of the cortex, striatum, GPi/SNr, and the thalamus, illustrating smooth and balanced communication between these regions. Unlike the Parkinsonian state, the healthy network displays controlled and synchronized activity, emphasizing the importance of dopamine in maintaining motor function. Comparing this with the previous figure highlights the stark contrast in how Parkinson’s Disease disrupts neural pathways.

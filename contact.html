Microfluidic Platforms for Advanced Cancer Cell Culturing and Tumor Microenvironment Modeling

Special thanks to Dr. Guillem Pratx for his support and guidance for the execution of this project.

Project Overview

This project focuses on the design, fabrication, and application of microfluidic devices to culture cancer cells and replicate the tumor microenvironment. Microfluidic platforms offer precise control over fluid flow, nutrient gradients, and chemical concentrations, making them ideal for mimicking the complex conditions in which tumors grow. Through 3D printing and advanced design techniques, we developed devices capable of sustaining cancer cell cultures, providing an environment that closely resembles in vivo tumor settings.

By engineering different microfluidic patterns and flow dynamics, we explored the interaction of cancer cells with their surrounding environment, including how cells respond to varying nutrient levels and therapeutic agents. This allows for more accurate modeling of drug responses and tumor growth, potentially advancing cancer research beyond traditional 2D cultures. Furthermore, these platforms facilitate the study of cancer cell migration, proliferation, and invasion in a controlled, reproducible manner.

Our work highlights the importance of integrating bioengineering with cancer biology to develop innovative tools for research. These devices not only provide a powerful platform for studying tumor biology but also pave the way for personalized medicine by testing patient-derived cells in similar microfluidic setups. The project’s outcome contributes to the ongoing effort to bridge the gap between preclinical studies and clinical applications, enhancing the reliability of cancer research and the development of new therapeutic strategies.

Figure 1: Microfluidic Device Design 1

This image showcases one of the microfluidic devices designed and 3D printed for culturing cancer cells. The intricate channel network is structured to ensure smooth fluid flow and uniform distribution of nutrients and cells. The design emphasizes the precision required in channel dimensions to avoid blockages while promoting consistent cell growth. This chip's compact and modular layout makes it suitable for single or multiplexed experiments, allowing for easy observation and data collection.

Figure 2: Microfluidic Device Design 2

This microfluidic chip features a more complex hexagonal pattern of chambers and channels. The design aims to enhance fluid dynamics by creating controlled environments for cell cultures. The individual wells facilitate parallel experiments, enabling comparisons under varying conditions, such as nutrient levels or drug dosages. This architecture also allows for efficient imaging and monitoring of cell behavior, making it ideal for high-throughput studies in cancer research.

Figure 3: Microfluidic Device in Action

This image showcases the microfluidic device connected to fluid delivery tubes, actively culturing cancer cells. The transparent design allows real-time monitoring of fluid flow and cell behavior. The setup demonstrates the precision required in delivering nutrients and maintaining flow rates, simulating dynamic physiological environments. The use of external tubing ensures continuous fluid exchange, which is essential for maintaining optimal cell growth conditions over time.

Figure 4: CAD Model of Microfluidic Device

This CAD model provides a comprehensive view of the microfluidic chip's intricate architecture. It highlights the network of parallel channels and connection points designed to achieve laminar flow. The model serves as the blueprint for 3D printing, ensuring high precision during fabrication. The modular design allows for multiple experimental configurations, offering flexibility in exploring various parameters affecting cancer cell growth and drug responses.

Figure 5: Mold for Microfluidic Device

This image showcases the 3D-printed mold used in the fabrication process of the microfluidic device. The mold contains intricate microchannel designs that facilitate controlled fluid flow when the device is cast. Precision in the mold’s design ensures accurate replication, which is essential for achieving optimal performance in cancer cell culture experiments.

Figure 6: Completed and Assembled Microfluidic Devices

This figure presents two microfluidic devices – one made of black polymer and the other from a transparent material. The transparent device is mounted on a glass slide, which is crucial for imaging and monitoring cell behavior under the microscope. These assembled devices allow for the continuous flow of media and nutrients, ensuring a suitable environment for the growth and analysis of cancer cells.

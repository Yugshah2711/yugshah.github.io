Optimization of Near-Dry Wire EDM for Nitinol SMA

Published in: Journal of Manufacturing and Materials Processing

DOI: 10.3390/jmmp060131

Project Overview

This study aimed to enhance the machining process of Nitinol SMA, a material known for its unique shape-memory properties and high potential in medical and aerospace applications. The focus was on optimizing the near-dry wire electrical discharge machining (WEDM) method, which uses minimal liquid for cooling and debris removal, reducing environmental impact. I conducted extensive experimental trials varying key parameters such as pulse on time, discharge current, and wire speed. These experiments helped assess critical output responses, including material removal rate (MRR), surface roughness, and recast layer thickness. The Taguchi-based Grey Relational Analysis (GRA) was applied to identify optimal machining conditions, balancing performance across multiple factors. Through this method, we significantly improved machining precision, achieving a 20% increase in MRR and a notable reduction in surface roughness and recast thickness. These findings have implications for industries that rely on high-precision machining, particularly in the biomedical sector where Nitinol’s biocompatibility and shape-memory properties are crucial. The optimized process minimizes surface damage, ensuring the integrity of machined components, which is essential for applications like stents and medical implants. My involvement extended from designing the experiments to data analysis and co-authoring the resulting publication.

Key Contributions

- Conducted multiple experimental trials with different parameters to optimize machining outcomes.
- Applied Taguchi-based GRA for multi-objective optimization to maximize MRR while minimizing surface roughness and recast layer thickness.
- Achieved a 20% increase in MRR, 15% reduction in surface roughness, and significant reduction in recast layer thickness, resulting in superior machining quality.
- Developed machining protocols that are particularly beneficial in biomedical applications requiring high precision and minimal surface damage.
